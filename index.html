Section 341 Meeting of Creditors

A plain English guide to the most important hearing in your bankruptcy case

If you have filed for bankruptcy - or are thinking about it - you have probably heard about the "341 meeting" or "meeting of creditors." It sounds intimidating. The name suggests a room full of the people you owe money to, grilling you about your finances. In reality, it is much simpler than that.

This guide explains what a Section 341 meeting actually is, what happens during one, and how to prepare so it goes as smoothly as possible.

What Is a 341 Meeting?

The 341 meeting gets its name from 11 U.S.C. Section 341, the part of the Bankruptcy Code that requires it. Every person who files for bankruptcy - whether Chapter 7, Chapter 13, or Chapter 11 - must attend a meeting of creditors. It is a mandatory part of every bankruptcy case in the United States.

Despite the name, this is not a courtroom hearing. The bankruptcy judge does not attend. In fact, Section 341(c) specifically prohibits the judge from being present. The meeting is run by the bankruptcy trustee assigned to your case.

The purpose is straightforward: the trustee needs to verify your identity, confirm that your bankruptcy paperwork is accurate, and ask questions about your financial situation. Creditors have the legal right to attend and ask questions too, but in most consumer cases, no creditors show up.

Who Attends the Meeting?

Always Present

  • You (the debtor) - your attendance is required by law under Section 343
  • The bankruptcy trustee - a court-appointed person who reviews your case
  • Your attorney - if you have one (and you should)

Sometimes Present

  • Creditors or their attorneys - they have the right to attend, but rarely do in straightforward cases
  • The U.S. Trustee's office - a branch of the Department of Justice that monitors bankruptcy cases for fraud or abuse
  • Other debtors - in many courts, several meetings are scheduled back-to-back in the same room or on the same Zoom call

What Happens During the Meeting?

The meeting follows a predictable pattern in most districts:

  1. Identity verification. The trustee checks your government-issued photo ID and proof of Social Security number. This is required to prevent fraud.
  2. Oath. You are placed under oath. Everything you say is under penalty of perjury. This is why accuracy on your petition matters.
  3. Standard questions. The trustee asks a set of routine questions: Did you list all your assets? Is your income accurate? Have you transferred property recently? Did you read and sign your petition?
  4. Follow-up questions. If something in your schedules raises a flag - an asset that seems undervalued, a recent large payment, unusual transactions - the trustee may ask more detailed questions.
  5. Creditor questions. If any creditors are present, they get a chance to ask questions. This is rare in simple consumer cases but more common in business bankruptcies or cases with significant assets.
  6. Conclusion or continuance. If the trustee has all the information needed, the meeting is concluded. If not, it may be continued to a later date so you can provide additional documents.

The whole process typically takes 5 to 15 minutes for a straightforward consumer case. You can read our detailed list of questions trustees commonly ask to prepare.

When Does the Meeting Happen?

The 341 meeting is usually scheduled 20 to 40 days after your bankruptcy petition is filed. The exact timing depends on your local court's calendar. You will receive an official notice from the court with the date, time, and location (or dial-in/Zoom information).

You cannot choose the date. If you have a conflict, your attorney can request a continuance, but it must be done before the meeting - not after you miss it.

Chapter 7 vs. Chapter 13: Is the Meeting Different?

The basic format is the same, but there are some differences:

  • Chapter 7: The trustee is looking to see if you have any non-exempt assets that can be sold to pay creditors. Questions focus on what you own and what it is worth.
  • Chapter 13: The trustee is focused on your repayment plan. Questions tend to center on your income, expenses, and whether your plan is feasible. The Chapter 13 trustee may ask about your budget in more detail.

In either case, the meeting is not adversarial. The trustee is not trying to trick you. They are trying to verify that your paperwork is accurate and complete.

Common Myths About the 341 Meeting

Myth: All your creditors will be there

In reality, creditors almost never attend in straightforward consumer cases. When they do, it is usually a mortgage company or car lender with a specific question about a secured debt.

Myth: You will be interrogated

The trustee asks standard questions. If your paperwork is accurate and complete, the questions are routine. It is more of a verification than an interrogation.

Myth: The judge decides your case at this meeting

The judge is not there. Section 341(c) prohibits it. Your discharge is a separate process that happens later.

Myth: If something goes wrong, your case is denied immediately

If the trustee needs more information, the meeting is continued - meaning rescheduled. You get another chance. Immediate denial at a 341 meeting is extremely rare.

How to Prepare

Preparation is the single best thing you can do. Here is a quick checklist:

  • Bring your required documents - ID, Social Security proof, tax returns, pay stubs
  • Review your bankruptcy petition before the meeting - make sure everything is accurate
  • Read through the common trustee questions so nothing catches you off guard
  • If your meeting is virtual, read our guide to Zoom and phone meetings
  • Arrive early (or log in early for virtual meetings)
  • Answer honestly - you are under oath

What Comes After the 341 Meeting?

After the meeting is concluded:

  • Chapter 7: If the trustee finds no issues, your case moves toward discharge. Creditors have 60 days from the first date set for the meeting to file objections. If none are filed, your discharge is typically entered about 90 days after the meeting.
  • Chapter 13: Your repayment plan confirmation hearing is usually scheduled shortly after the 341 meeting. If the trustee approves your plan and no creditors object, the court confirms it and you begin making payments.

If your case was dismissed in a prior bankruptcy and you are trying to file again, timing rules under Section 1328(f) may affect your eligibility for discharge. Our free screener at 1328f.com can help you check.

Frequently Asked Questions

What questions do they ask at a 341 meeting?

The trustee will ask you to confirm your identity, verify your petition is accurate, and answer questions about your income, assets, debts, and recent financial transactions. Common questions include: Do you own any real estate? Have you transferred any property in the last two years? Are your schedules complete and accurate? Is all your income listed? The questions are usually straightforward and take about 5 to 10 minutes. See our full list of trustee questions.

How long does a 341 meeting last?

Most 341 meetings last between 5 and 15 minutes. If your paperwork is complete and there are no complications, it can be as quick as 5 minutes. More complex cases - those involving real estate, business assets, or potential preference payments - may take longer.

Can I do my 341 meeting by phone or Zoom?

Many courts now offer 341 meetings by phone or video conference. This became widespread during COVID-19 and many districts have kept it. Check your court's notice for the format. Read our complete guide to virtual 341 meetings.

What happens if I miss my 341 meeting?

Missing your 341 meeting is serious. The trustee will likely request that your case be dismissed. In most courts, the meeting will be rescheduled once, but if you miss it again, dismissal is almost certain. Read our full guide on missing a 341 meeting.

What documents do I need to bring?

You must bring a government-issued photo ID and proof of your Social Security number. Many trustees also require your most recent tax return, recent pay stubs, and bank statements. See our complete document checklist.
